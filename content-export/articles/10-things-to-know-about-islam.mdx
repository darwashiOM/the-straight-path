ar_body: '## 1. Islam is the religion of all creation

Islam is the natural way of life, based on the voluntary submission of the human being to God. This submission begins in the heart, as a person recognizes Allāh as the ultimate source of all provision. When a Muslim eats a bite of food, they recognize that it is Allāh who causes the sun to rise, the rain to fall, and the earth to produce its sustenance.

This belief naturally extends into action: all acts of worship are directed to Allāh alone. This includes formal acts such as prayer and fasting, as well as everyday goodness — kindness to family, honesty in dealings, and charity to those in need.

In this submission, a Muslim follows the path of all true believers throughout history. Noah, Abraham, Moses, Jesus, and Muḥammad — peace be upon them all — called to the same essential message: worship Allāh alone and do good. In this sense, they are all part of one continuous tradition of submission to God: Islam.

## 2. A clear and coherent creed

The core belief of Islam is simple and unambiguous: there is one God without partners or children. He is unique in His attributes, and nothing resembles Him.

He sent messengers to guide humanity to the truth. He is perfectly just and does not burden any soul beyond what it can bear. Each person is accountable only for their own deeds, and Islam rejects the concept of inherited sin. Adam and Eve''s mistake was forgiven by Allāh, the Most Merciful.

## 3. Islam transforms the heart

Revelation in Islam is not only guidance for outward behavior; it reshapes the inner life. It introduces sincerity (*ikhlāṣ*) as a central principle of worship.

While it is natural for people to seek recognition, Islam teaches that Allāh accepts actions only when they are done purely for His sake. This purifies intention and reorients motivation. Charity, for example, is given to seek the pleasure of Allāh and to help those in need — not to be seen or praised by others.

This sincerity produces consistency: good deeds remain steady even when unnoticed. It also enables quiet acts of goodness done privately, with reward sought only from Allāh.

## 4. A religion of refined character

The Prophet Muḥammad said:

> "I guarantee a house in the outskirts of Paradise for one who leaves argument even when he is right; a house in the middle of Paradise for one who leaves lying even in jest; and a house in the highest part of Paradise for one who perfects his character."

In Islam, honesty is not situational. Even small, joking lies are discouraged, shaping a person who is truthful in all circumstances.

## 5. A relatable model of excellence

The Prophet Muḥammad was not only a historical figure, but a complete example of human character.

As a child, he was resilient. As a young man, he was known for his trustworthiness. As a husband, he was affectionate and forbearing. As a father, he was gentle and patient. As a prophet, he was sincere and devoted. As a leader, he was just and merciful.

He experienced poverty and persecution, as well as authority and victory. He lived through hardship and ease, conflict and peace. In every stage of life, he embodied moral excellence in action.

## 6. A perfectly preserved revelation

The Qur''ān has been preserved word-for-word in its original Arabic since its revelation. It is not only preserved in writing, but also through continuous oral transmission, memorized in full by generation after generation.

Across the Muslim world, large numbers of people transmit the same text independently through verified chains of narration. This widespread, parallel preservation makes alteration extremely implausible.

Muslims believe this preservation is ultimately safeguarded by Allāh, who promised to protect the Qur''ān.

## 7. A grounded sense of purpose

Islam cultivates resilience by shaping how a believer responds to life''s events. A Muslim strives to remain grateful in times of ease and patient in times of hardship.

This perspective is built on several principles: Allāh is aware of every difficulty; what appears harmful may carry hidden good; hardship can serve as a means of spiritual purification; trials are temporary; and others before us have endured similar struggles.

With this understanding, a believer navigates life with purpose. Hardship does not break him, and depression does not take hold of his heart. He may grieve, but proceeds with resolve towards his final abode.

## 8. A comprehensive way of life

Islam is not limited to rituals of worship. It provides guidance across all areas of life, including how a person eats, sleeps, earns, marries, raises children, and interacts with others.

Rather than separating spirituality from daily life, Islam integrates moral and spiritual awareness into every action, making it a complete framework for living.

## 9. Unity in diversity

When Muslims gather for prayer, they stand side by side — rich and poor, young and old, from every race and background — united in worship of one Creator.

The Qur''ān states:

> "O mankind, We created you from a male and a female and made you into peoples and tribes so that you may know one another. The most noble of you in the sight of Allāh is the most righteous." (49:13)

This unity is supported by a strong emphasis on family structure. Islam preserves the institution of marriage by prohibiting intimacy outside of it. It encourages modesty and dignity — women adorn themselves within the privacy of the home, and both men and women are taught to lower their gaze and carry themselves with respect.

## 10. A message that engages the mind

The first revelation of the Qur''ān begins:

> "Read in the Name of your Lord Who created — created the human being from a clinging clot. Read! And your Lord is the Most Generous, Who taught by the pen — taught the human being what he did not know."

From its beginning, the Qur''ān calls people to reflection on the natural world, human origins, and the pursuit of knowledge — encouraging observation, reasoning, and learning.

This intellectual spirit is reflected in the legacy of early Muslim scholars. In the introduction to *The Compendious Book on Calculation by Completion and Balancing*, Al-Khwārizmī praises Allāh and explains that his work was written to solve practical problems in Islamic inheritance law. He viewed beneficial knowledge as a form of worship. His work went on to lay the foundations of what is now known as algebra.'
